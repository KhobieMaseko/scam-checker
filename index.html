10 rules that keep you safe

Follow these practices and you will avoid the overwhelming majority of scams

💸

Never pay to receive money

Legitimate prizes, inheritances, and job offers never require an upfront payment. This is the single most reliable scam signal that exists.

⏱️

Resist artificial urgency

"Act in 24 hours or lose your prize" is a pressure tactic. Real opportunities do not evaporate overnight. Urgency is manufactured to stop you thinking clearly.

🔗

Verify links before clicking

Hover over any link to see the real URL. Scammers use domains like amazon-support.xyz or paypa1.com. When in doubt, go directly to the official website.

📞

Hang up and call back officially

If a "bank" or "government agency" calls you, hang up and call the official number on their website or your card. Never trust the number someone gives you.

🔒

Use unique passwords + 2FA

A password manager and two-factor authentication on every account eliminates the majority of account takeover scams before they even start.

💳

Crypto and gift cards = scam

No legitimate business, government agency, or utility company will ever ask you to pay via cryptocurrency or gift cards. This is 100% a scam payment method.

🤳

Verify job offers independently

Search the company name + "scam" or "reviews." Look up the company on LinkedIn. Call their official number. Legitimate employers expect due diligence.

❤️

Be cautious online romances

If someone you've never met in person asks for money, it's a romance scam — regardless of how long you've been talking. Do a reverse image search on their photos.

📧

Check the sender's actual email

The display name can say "Amazon" but the address can be random@gmail.com. Always expand the sender info and look at the actual email domain.

📣

Report every scam you catch

Report to reportfraud.ftc.gov (US), your national cybercrime authority, and right here. Every report makes the next person safer.

Frequently asked questions

Everything you need to know about the tool

How do I know if a message is a scam? +
Paste the message into the checker on this page. The AI analyses the text for known scam patterns and returns a verdict — Scam, Likely Scam, Suspicious, or Likely Safe — along with the specific red flags found in your actual message and the exact steps you should take. The whole process takes under 10 seconds.
Is this tool really free? +
Yes, completely. No account required, no credit card, no usage limits for standard analysis. It was built to be free because the people who need it most are often the ones who can least afford to pay. If it has helped you, consider supporting us on Ko-fi to keep it running.
What types of scams can it detect? +
The tool detects phishing emails, job scams, romance scams, investment and crypto fraud, lottery and prize scams, government impersonation, tech support scams, rental scams, advance-fee fraud (419), brand impersonation (Amazon, PayPal, banks), and social engineering. If you paste something that doesn't fit a known category, it will still flag unusual pressure tactics and suspicious language patterns.
Is my message stored when I check it? +
Only the first 500 characters are anonymously saved to the public scam database so the community can learn from real examples. Your full message is never stored. Never paste passwords, bank account numbers, ID numbers, or any sensitive personal information into the tool.
How accurate is the AI verdict? +
The tool is powered by Llama 3.3 70B via Groq and is highly accurate on common scam types with clear patterns. It is less reliable on very short messages or highly context-dependent situations. Always treat the verdict as a strong starting point for your own judgment — not a final ruling. If in doubt, do not send money or share personal information regardless of what the tool says.
Can I look up a phone number or email address? +
Yes. Use the Lookup tab to search an email address, phone number, username, or cryptocurrency wallet address against the ScamSearch.io community database of known reported scammers. A result there is a strong warning sign — no result means it has not been reported yet, which does not guarantee it is safe.
What should I do if I have already been scammed? +
Act quickly. Contact your bank immediately if money was transferred. Report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov (US), Action Fraud (UK), or ScamWatch (Australia). If personal information was shared, place a fraud alert with the credit bureaus. Do not send additional money to "recover" what was lost — that is always a secondary scam.
